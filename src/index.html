Trambar

est. 2018

What is Trambar?

Trambar is a companion tool for GitLab. It lets you maintain a Facebook-like newsfeed chronicling your development effort. Stories are automatically extracted from a repo's activity log. Users can also add their own stories, adorned with photos and videos.

Trambar is meant to be a low-stakes, informal environment, a place when programmers and non-programmers alike can feel at home.

The backend of Trambar is an easy to deploy Docker app. The frontend runs on all modern browsers. It's also available as an app on Android, iOS, and Windows Mobile.

Marginally useful information

Getting people to look at what they would otherwise overlook--that's Trambar's main purpose. IT workers are typically goal-oriented. They pay attention to information that helps them achieve their goals and ignore information that doesn't.

Not useful is not the same as useless though. Information that fails to meet people's attention threshold often has some value. Cumulatively, it can add up to quite a bit.

Git activity logs fall into this category. Knowledge that a push occurred is rarely useful to programmers, let alone those who don't code. It does indicate, however, that progress is being made. Seeing a steady stream of pushes can be reassuring, especially for team members who're not programmers. After all, their livelihoods too are tied to the code. Not knowing how this "thing" is evolving would otherwise leads to endless anxiety.

Information of uncertain value

Unlike automation tools, people have a sense of shame. They won't continually create materials that inevitably get ignored. They will try to send only useful information to their colleagues.

Doing so is easy when the information was first requested. When information is unsolicited, judging its value becomes hard. The sender is unsure of the recipient's wants and needs.

For example, someone makes a suggestion on a software feature. If the idea ends up in the final product, the information has a positive value. If the suggestion is ignored, its value is zero. If it involves substantial changes and causes an overworked programmer to blow a gasket, its value is negative.

Trambar's low-stakes nature makes it a good place for this sort of information.

Relocating distractions

Issue tracker is a high-stakes system, central to a project's workflow. Quality of information there ought to be high. When it's filled with unripened ideas and vague observations of software misbehavior, its usefulness is degraded.

Likewise, instant messenger is high-stakes, due to the implicit demand for immediate response. When its channels are filled with information that isn't actually time critical, people will start to ignore it. The tool then no longer fulfills its purpose.

Trambar can compliment these frontline systems by hoovering up all the low-value information.

Chicken or the egg conundrum

Attempts to segregate information by priority often run into the chicken or the egg conundrum. People don't post things because they're convinced no one would look at them. Meanwhile, people don't visit the site because no one is posting there.

Timing is everything

When people are working, they don't want to be distracted. That's especially true for programmers, whose work demand deep concentration.

When people are bored, on the other hand, they want to be distracted. Starving for mental stimulation, they're perfectly willing to spend time looking at what their friends had for lunches.

Envoking the right mood

Mobile considerations

Trambar is meant to be used when people are bored. This usually happens when they're not in front of their computers. The mobile phone is therefore a very key platform.

Losses of mobile devices are unavoidable. This assumption is built into Trambar's design. Trambar does not import data from GitLab that could lead to security breaches. Commit messages are omitted and no code ever appears. The mobile app also does not permit signing in using GitLab credentials.

Omission of sensitive materials means Trambar sessions can be long living. That allows people to check the newsfeed quickly and frequently, without the hassle of entering a password. Trambar is designed to work in windows of time less than 30 seconds.

Mixing work with pleasure

The Trambar newsfeed is designed to accommodate non-work-related materials. In fact, it depends on their presence since they provide the necessary attraction.

One can think of git events on Trambar as ads on Facebook. People aren't keen to look at them. If a newsfeed has nothing but ads, people would avoid it like the plague. When ads are interspersed among user-generated contents, people will spare them half a second or two. An instant versus never makes a world of difference.

Expanding the social circle

Software development usually involves many people who are not programmers. Testers, tech writers, and managers are some of the more common roles. Trambar helps you keep these other stakeholders in the loop.

While their jobs might not be code-related, whether they will have their jobs in the future does depend very much on the code. It's entirely natural that they want to see evidence the project is on track. The Trambar newsfeed lets you provide this necessary reassurance at a very low cost.

Trambar is especially useful when team members are separated geographically, a situation where trust tends to lacking and a poisonous them-verus-us mentality can easily develop.

Creating sense out of diff's

Trambar features a simple, flexible source-tree decoration system. Component descriptions, written from end-user prespective, are mapped to source files using .gitignore rules. When a push or merge occurs, users will see a list of components affected. This is more intuitive and less intimidating to non-programmers than a file diff plus commit messages.

These descriptions can also serve as a roadmap for onboarding programmers.
